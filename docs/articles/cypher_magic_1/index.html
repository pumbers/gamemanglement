Cypher System: Elemental Magic

  • Oct 28, 2018
  • ARTICLES

My love of the Cypher System has been a rocky road (as in rough, not the ice cream). Back when I first laid hands on a copy of Numenera (1st edition), I read it and didn’t understand it. I loved the background and storylines - and bear in mind here I’ve been playing role-playing games since Classic Traveller at University in England. I still didn’t get the game system. My gut told me it worked and it was very good, but my head didn’t get it. So I put it on one side for a while.

Then when the Cypher System Rulebook came out, I grabbed a copy. I read it, loved it, but still didn’t get it. Or rather I understood the basics but I didn’t see how Cyphers could be at all versatile enough to be used in so many different ways across so many different game worlds.

One of the things I disliked (intensely) about Cypher - and still do to this day - is the randomness and capriciousness with which magic is spread across the types and foci. The Adept class and Magic Flavor seemed to be a hodgepodge of magical abilities with no real pattern or order to them. The same goes for some non-magical abilities, but I can live with those because there’s not so many of them.

Why should I be able to Erase Memories or do Hedge Magic at First-Tier? How come I can’t create barriers until Third-Tier? Why do I have to wait until Sixth-Tier to cause earthquakes? How is erasing memories related to creating earthquakes. It didn’t make sense.

I wanted to create a meta-magic system for Cypher which (i) fits with the game mechanics of cyphers and artifacts, (ii) had some sort of order and reasoning behind it so that players could create their own magical effects from the building blocks provided and (iii) allowed characters at any tier to create potentially significant effects if they wanted to risk it.

I puzzled over it for some time. I hate spell lists, they take forever to write (and besides, most are nth generation copies of OSR spell lists anyway). I hate Vancian Magic, just the idea of forgetting a spell once it’s cast seems very Discworld to me (or is it the other way around). Not inherently bad, but I don’t like it.

And then I happened upon the Classical Elements Expansion by Matthew Barber (aka Honno, a University student living in my old hometown of Birmingham, UK).

I liked it, so I contacted Matt and got his permission to use the idea in an RPG.

It had the same elements (pun intended) as classical elemental magic - Earth, Air, Fire and Water referred to as first-order or Fundamental elements - but then (as the name suggests) it expands on the available elements by mixing these fundamentals into second-order Terrestrial elements and adding third-order Celestial elements. The fourth-order elements are usually referred to as Origin and are the source of all magic. In game terms, magic can have any origin - I’m not telling you what mine is because that would just ruin it for the players and isn’t really important yet.

NOW I had more than the basic four elements to play with and could start to produce some serious meta-magic. Here we go. Not much Cypher System stuff yet, just the basics of the magic system.

Magic 101

Magic is elemental in nature. Everything that was, is and will be is built from combinations of the fourteen elements on the chart. The material world is composed of the fundamental and terrestrial elements while the immaterial world, the world we cannot see but is the structure on which consciousness is built, is composed of the celestial elements.

Finding Pure Elemental Forms

Most of what you see around you contains so little pure elemental form that it is considered mundane. A stream is just water, a campfire is just fire and earth is just dirt. But, every now and then those skilled in the ways of magic, those who have affinity with particular elements can find a sign, a cynosure of the pure element.

Using Elemental Forms

Once a cynosure has been found, its elemental form can be gathered and either chanelled or bound.

An Adept can tap into the form, gather it’s energies and channel them immediately and directly. Think: find a fire cynosure, stick your hand in it, throw a fireball, the elemental form is gone (along with whatever you threw the fireball at, probably). This tends to be risky and barely controlled with potentially bad side-effects - such as a burned hand or other form of fatigue or damage - but it is direct and effective.

The alternative is to bind the form, either to a suitable inanimate object or to a living being.

Binding to an object requires something which, in elemental terms, has a relationship or correspondance with the element being bound. A small glass vial, for example, can be used to bind a water or air form, a tinderbox can hold a fire form. A wood form can be attached to an existing object, say the hilt of a sword or a staff. At the time the form is bound, the Adept has a choice: they can either bind the raw form or create an expression.

Binding the raw form does just that - the element is bound and can be tapped into at any point in the future to create a magical effect in a manner similar to chanelling BUT this can only be done by an Adept who has affinity for the element. Adepts without affinity for that element can sense it’s power, but do nothing with it. To anyone else, it’s just a dumb old object.

Creating an expression binds the element to the object and fashions it into a pattern for creating a specific effect which can later be called forth by anyone who knows the trigger the Adept imprinted at the time of binding. Triggers can be anything the Adept desires: words, somatic gestures, the presence of other elements or objects. Your choice. Take a fire form, bind it to a charcoal rod and imprint it with a codeword or phrase - now you have a wand of fireballs (in Cypher terms, single use only).

An elemental form can also be bound to a person or other living being and the binding can be either raw or expressed.

Needless to say, binding a raw elemental form to a living being is risky for both parties (but particularly the recipient). A raw elemental form is like an unexploded bomb being held in check only by the recipient’s Intellect. If the recipient is ever knocked unconscious or dies, the elemental form is released in a chaotic fashion (GMs fiat) - sleep is OK though as the subconscious keeps the form in check. If the recipient is an Adept: congratulations - you now have a raw elemental form on tap whenever you need it. Handy. If the recipient is not an Adept: congratulations, any Adepts around you have access to a raw elemental form whenever they need it (even if you don’t want them to).

How about binding to a living being that is NOT considered sentient (0 Intellect, effectively)? Can’t happen. There must be some form of sentience or consciousness sufficient to receive, understand and contain the element and most animals (for example) don’t have that. The binding will simply fail with the form being released (perhaps catastrophically) in the process.

When an elemental form is bound to a person and expressed, as with an inanimate object, the Adept must decide on the effect and trigger at the time of binding. This form can then only be triggered by the recipient and will not trigger accidentally if the recipient is knocked unconscious or dies (unless that was the trigger). Much, much safer.

Effects

The effect that an elemental form produces is primarily related to the fundamental nature of the element and will never produce an effect related to its opposing element on the chart. Freeze tends to produce some form of cold, for example, and you can’t get a Magma form to produce ice. An elemental form also has a secondary effect related to its Transcendental State. Spark, for example, has the state Kinetic so could be used to produce a “Haste” or rapid movement effect (increasing the kinetic energy of an object or person). Steam has the state Ascend and so could be used to produce some form of levitation while its antithesis, Dust, has the state Fall which could produce the opposite effect.

Finally, whenever an Adept has two or more first-order elemental forms available to them they can attempt to combine them together to produce a second or third-order form. For example, let’s say an Adept has a Fire form and Air form bound to himself. He can choose to manifest them and combine them (according to the chart) to produce a Steam form which has the transcendental state Ascend - hey, instant levitation spell.

Limitations

The world is both shaped by and shapes magic.

In their “natural” environment pure elemental forms and their cynosures will usually be found in small numbers and quantities. The effect that a concentration of magic has on the environment is what creates the cynosure in the first place and nature balances out these concentrations through a form of elemental osmosis that tends to spread the energy around when it becomes too concentrated.

Any time there is more than one elemental form bound to a single person or that person is carrying more than one bound object, there may be a problem, however. What keeps the magic in check is willpower or Intellect.

The act of consciously carrying one or more bound forms is sufficient to keep them in check, at least up to a limit of a handful per person. In game terms, this is where the cypher limit comes from. Carry too many cyphers and a GM intrusion may result in an unintended, catastrophic side effect being generated by one or more of those cyphers. GM fiat again.

If you’re carrying a bound form in your body, the cynosure reasserts itself and physical changes occur which the recipient has some control over at the time of binding. Most often, these are made to appear as tattoos, sigils or other marks on the skin which cannot be erased until the form is used - they can be in a place obscured by clothing or hair though. Sometimes, they can manifest as physiological changes: hair color, eye color, skin color, a different texture or appearance to the skin and so on.

Anyone carrying a bound form can, at any time, simply release the element from its binding. All this takes is a few seconds conscious thought BUT they will have almost no control over how the element behaves when it is released. That pesky GM fiat again. Releasing an air element might result in a sudden strong gust of wind which blows everyone around, a fire form might cause a blast of intense heat, and so on.

Summary

So, now I have the basis of an elemental magic system which is flexible - almost any effect can be created - and is not tied to random abilities at given tiers. It works with the game system - cyphers, cypher limits, artifacts, etc and it has the opportunity to create a wide variety of in-game effects as intrusions of one form or another. It does rely on the players and GM working together to ensure effects don’t get out of hand, but that’s fine and in the spirit of Cypher.

Future articles will cover how this can be implemented within the Cypher System, including the Adept Type and Magic Flavor implementations.
